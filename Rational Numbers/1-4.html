Unit Conversion

Rational Numbers

2021-06-22

Concept

Units are always a bit of a complex topic because it's easy to get confused on what an abbreviation means, whether to multiply or divide by a conversion factor, or even what unit your currently in. Despite all that, units give meaning to mathematical results - they tell us what our numbers mean.

Unit Basics

Let's get some terminology straightened out.

Units are always a fraction. Even if it's just the numerator or denominator, they are a fraction and should be treated as such.

Each unit belongs to a system and has a category. A category represents what the units measures - distance, time, weight, etc. A system represents a group of standardized units and the conversion factors between them. Conversion factors allow you to convert between units of the same category. Let's look at an example.

The foot belongs to the customary system and it measures length (its category). The conversion factor from feet to miles is \(\frac{1}{5280}\), because there is 1 mile for every 5280 feet. Similarily, the conversion factor from miles to feet is reversed (or rather, reciprocated): \(\frac{5280}{1}\) because there are 5280 feet for every mile.

When units are taken to some power \(n\), they usually represent measuring that unit in \(n\) dimensions. That's just a fancy way to say that length squared means area (length in 2d). Similarly, length to the 3rd power is volume (length in 3d - also mostly for solids).

Conversions

Let's talk a bit more about converting between units. There's a very simple technique to follow to ensure you convert properly and you get to keep track of all the units.

First, remember that when multiplying fractions, items diagonally across each other can cancel out if they're the same or share factors. For example, \(\frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{5}{2} = \frac{2 \cdot 5}{3 \cdot 2}\). Since we have a two on both the top and bottom we can cancel it out. We don't even have to multiply the fractions to see that we can - they're diagonally across from each other. Canceling the twos gives us \(\frac{5}{3}\).

Now, remember that units are fractions - so we can do the same thing. If a car is going at 120 miles per hour (someone's getting a speeding ticket), how fast is it going in miles per minute?

Well, we'll start with what we're given. We'll write like a fence (hence the name fence method) instead of repeatedly writing fractions. The multiplication between the columns of the fence is assumed - it's the same thing as before, just a better format.

\[ \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{0.8} \begin{array}{c|} 120 \mbox{ miles} \\ \hline 1 \mbox{ hour} \\ \end{array} \]

Now we want to convert our hours into minutes, since we want miles per minute. We know that there are 60 minutues for every hour. To align it properly, we need to cancel out the hours, which means putting the hours on top in the next column. Since hours is on the bottom in the first column, hours cancels out and we're left with miles on top and minutes on the bottom.

\[ \begin{array}{c|c|} 120 \mbox{ miles} & 1 \mbox{ hour}\\ \hline 1 \mbox{ hour} & 60 \mbox{ minutes}\\ \end{array} \]

To get our final answer just multiply all the numbers on the top and divide by the numbers on the bottom.

\[ \begin{array}{c|c|c} 120 \mbox{ miles} & 1 \cancel{\mbox{ hour}} & 2 \mbox{ miles}\\ \hline 1 \cancel{\mbox{ hour}} & 60 \mbox{ minutes} & 1 \mbox{ minute}\\ \end{array} \]

Easy peasy.

Unit Systems

Most, if not all, standardized units you'll use will fall under with the customary (read: America) system or the SI - System Internationale, or metric, system. Let's compare them, and then examine the basics of each system.

Customary Metric
Used commonly Used for science
Incremental units have random conversion factors Incremental units have conversion factors in powers of 10
Defined by actual things Defined by standardized numbers

The Customary System

This system contains many familiar units, like feet, inches, gallons, quarts and etc. While we deal with it more often, it's usually harder to deal with in math because of the unusual conversion factors.

With length, the important units are inches, feet, yards and miles. There are 12 inches in a foot, 3 feet in a yard and 5280 feet in a mile.

With volume, if we're talking about liquids, we have 3 teaspoons in a tablespoon, 2 tablespoons into a fluid ounce, 8 fluid ounces in a cup, 2 cups in a pint, 2 pints in a quart and 4 quarts in a gallon. You'll wish you were using metric soon.

With weight, the customary system works in pounds.

The Metric System

This system might not be so familiar but is a lot easier to work with.

The metric system has a few base units: the second, the meter, the second, the ampere, the kelvin, the mole, and the candela. Every other unit imaginable is some combination of these 7. These base 7 units can't be expressed in terms of each other - making them the pillars that hold the system up.

You might notice in the customary system, we have different units to represent different magnitudes - both feet and miles measure length just at different scales. The conversion factors in the customary system aren't standardized between such units, but in the metric system, they're based on powers of 10.

For example, the meter. If we need to talk about thousands of meters, it's inconvenient and difficult to repeatedly write such magnitudes. Instead 1000m becomes 1km (kilometer). With length, we have the meter and its prefixes.

With volume, we have the liter and its prefixes.

With weight, we have the gram and its prefixes - but this measures mass. Weight depends on gravity - your mass is the same on the moon and the earth - but your weight is less on the moon because its gravity is lower. Since most everything you deal with in on earth, you can get away with using just the one conversion factor you'll see later on.

Converting between Metric and Customary

Let's talk about some conversion factors between the systems.

To convert length, 1 inch is equivalent to 2.54 cm.

To convert volume, 1 gallon is equivalent to 3.78 L.

To convert weight, 1 pound is equivalent to 0.454 kg.

To convert from mass to volume in the metric system, 1 cubic center is equivalent to a milliliter.
