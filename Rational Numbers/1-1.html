Negative Numbers

Rational Numbers

2021-06-01

Concept

This shouldn't be too hard to understand (I hope), but let's go over it either way.

Imagine a number line with just the positive numbers. The positive numbers go off to the right forever and ever. At the very left of this original number line is 0. Negative numbers simply extend the number line in the other direction - going left for eternity.

This makes for some interesting changes compared to positive numbers. Which number is smaller: \(1\) or \(2\)? \(1\). But which negative number is smaller: \(-1\) or \(-2\)? \(-2\). A number being smaller means it's farther left on the number line, not closer to \(0\).

The absolute value of a number represents how close it is to \(0\). To get the absolute value of a number, simply strip it of its sign. \(\lvert 2 \rvert\) is \(2\) and \(\lvert -2 \rvert\) is also \(2\). This makes sense, as both numbers are the same distance away from \(0\) on the number line.

Operations

Now let's talk about operations with a negative number.

Let's start with addition. Adding a negative number and a negative number will always be negative. You're taking something less than \(0\) and making it more less than \(0\). Adding a negative number to a positive number or vice versa depends on the absolute values of the numbers. If the positive number has a greater absolute value, the answer will be positive, and if the negative number has a greater absolute value than the answer will be negative.

With subtraction, things can get a bit confusing. A simple rule of thumb is to turn subtraction into addition by applying the negative sign to the following number. Take \(5 - (-2)\) for example. We'll take the minus sign, turn it into a plus, and apply the negative to the \(-2\). Now we have \(5+2\), once the negatives cancel out. Nice.

As a general rule of thumb, subtracting a negative number is like adding a positive one with the same absolute value.

With multiplication and division, cancellation is the name of the game. A negative number times or divided by a positive one (or the other way around) will always give you a negative number. A negative number times or divided by another negative number is always positive. Two positives always give you a positive. Just keep track of your signs and you'll be alright.

Speed

Negative numbers are ubiquitous in math. Practice the rules, know them well, and negative numbers will become second nature just as positive numbers are. Drawing a number line is always useful if you find yourself a bit confused, and be sure to properly write down your signs and operators clearly.
